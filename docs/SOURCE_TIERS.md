# Source Tier Reference (for nonpartisan citation discipline)

Every factual claim in research output must cite its source inline. Use only Tier 1–3 sources. Tier 4 advocacy sources are permitted only when explicitly labeled as advocacy. Format inline citations as `[Source: NAME]` for Tier 1–3 and `[Advocacy: NAME]` for Tier 4. If no Tier 1–3 source supports a claim, drop the claim — do not soften it, do not paraphrase from memory, do not guess.

## Tier 1 — Government and academic data (highest credibility)

Federal:

- Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) — employment, wages
- Bureau of Justice Statistics (BJS) and FBI Uniform Crime Reports (UCR) — crime
- Centers for Disease Control (CDC) — health
- US Census Bureau — demographics, economic indicators
- US Department of Education (ED) — education data

Texas-specific:

- Texas Demographic Center
- Texas Department of Public Safety (DPS) — crime
- Texas Education Agency (TEA) — education
- Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC)
- Texas Ethics Commission (TEC) — campaign finance filings
- County open data portals (Harris, Travis, Dallas, Bexar)

## Tier 2 — Academic and peer-reviewed

- University research centers
- JAMA, NEJM, and other peer-reviewed health journals
- Criminology, education policy, and economics journals

## Tier 3 — Think tanks and research orgs

Use carefully and balance across leans:

- Pew Research (center, methodologically strong)
- RAND Corporation (nonpartisan, rigorous)
- Urban Institute (center-left, methodologically solid)
- Brookings (center-left) and AEI (center-right) — paired together if used
- Vera Institute (criminal justice, advocacy-adjacent — label clearly)
- Tax Foundation (right-lean, but rigorous on tax policy)

## Tier 4 — Advocacy groups

Permitted only when labeled. Examples: ACLU, Heritage Foundation, Texas Public Policy Foundation, Center for American Progress. Always label as `[Advocacy: NAME]` so the voter knows the source has a position. Never present an advocacy source as neutral data.

## Candidate behavior data

These sources track candidate-specific actions and are nonpartisan in posture:

- Vote Smart (key votes, positions, ratings)
- Ballotpedia (race details, voting records, biographies)
- OpenSecrets (federal campaign finance)
- Texas Ethics Commission filings (state-level campaign finance)
- The candidate's own published votes/statements (label as `[Source: candidate's own statements]` so the voter knows it's self-reported)
- Established local newspapers (e.g., Houston Chronicle, Dallas Morning News, Austin American-Statesman, San Antonio Express-News) — label as `[Source: NEWSPAPER NAME, DATE]`

## Citation pattern

Inline. Every claim. No exceptions:

- "Travis County saw a 12% drop in property crime year-over-year [Source: FBI UCR 2025]."
- "This candidate received $48,000 from oil and gas PACs in 2024 [Source: TEC filings]."
- "Civil rights groups have flagged this voting record as restrictive [Advocacy: ACLU Texas]."

## When sources disagree or evidence is thin

Say so explicitly. Do not paper over disagreement with vague language. Example: "Two studies disagree on whether [policy] reduces recidivism — [Source: RAND 2023] found a small effect, [Source: Urban Institute 2024] found none. The evidence is contested."

## Spanish

When the conversation is in Spanish, translate source labels naturally (`[Fuente: NAME]`, `[Defensa: NAME]`) but keep the source name in its original form.
